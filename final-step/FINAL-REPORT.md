The most significant variation in file structure, code quality and performance occured in the first phase. This is likely due to the brevity and open ended nature of the prompt, allowing for a wide range of acceptable approaches for the llm. The majority of the websites were functional, yet not perfect, the most common errors we noticed being the quality of the animations when spinning, as well as the win loss functionality. However one thing that was strangely consistent given the lack of direction in the prompt was the aesthetic theme / design of the websites. Almost all of the websites looked very similar, sharing the same color themes, emoticon choices and general design. This surprised us considering the fact that there is no direction in the original prompt that should really influence those kinds of decision making, and all of the websites were made completely independantly of each other in terms of claude sessions. In fact, these similarities persisted between websites that were generated from completely different computers and claude accounts entirely (just as another sanity check to convince ourselves there was no influence). 
For the other stages, we tried alternate approaches to the prompts (in the different phases), some phases focusing on more specific smaller context improvements, and others being more general complete revamps more similar to the initial prompt. We ended up concluding that the more concise, incremental prompts tended to have more effective results. Larger more open ended tasks, took a disproportionate amount of time, and resources (token consumption) for what appeared to be minimal changes. The more specific we got, the better claude was at both accomplishing that goal, and doing it faster.
